How we actually researched these schools

Most school comparison sites list what schools say about themselves. We went further — cross-checking every claim against independent sources, reading what parents and teachers actually say, and flagging where marketing doesn't match reality.

The problem we're solving

When we started researching schools for our own children, we noticed something frustrating. Every school website says the same things — "holistic development," "world-class infrastructure," "child-centric learning." After visiting the tenth school that claimed to be progressive but had 35 kids per class doing worksheets, we decided to build something better.

Most comparison sites pull data from school self-submissions. That's like reading a resumé and calling it a background check. We wanted the background check.

How we score each school

Every school is evaluated on 10 parameters — from affordability and student-teacher ratio to how open the system is to parent feedback. But the interesting part isn't what we score. It's how we verify.

1

Start with public claims

School websites, brochures, Ezyschooling listings. What does the school say about itself? This gives us the baseline — but we treat it as unverified.

2

Cross-check against independent sources

Google Reviews, Justdial ratings, SchoolMyKids, YellowSlate, MouthShut, UrbanPro — each platform has a different audience and moderation style. When three independent platforms agree, we have signal. When they disagree, we dig deeper.

3

Read what teachers say

Glassdoor and Indeed employee reviews tell us about work culture — because schools that treat teachers poorly eventually teach poorly. A school with high teacher turnover can't build deep relationships with your child.

4

Look for what's NOT being said

A school that only shows board toppers on its website is telling you something about its priorities. A school with a skating rink and an amphitheatre is telling you something different. We read between the lines — systematically.

5

Flag contradictions

When a school markets itself as "holistic" but multiple parents independently mention heavy homework loads — that's a red flag. We surface these, with source attribution, so you can decide what matters to you.

Reading between the lines

Some of the most useful information about a school isn't stated directly — it's implied by what you can observe. We developed a systematic framework for interpreting these signals:

🏊

Swimming pool, skating rink, amphitheatre

School invests beyond academics. Values whole-child development. Less likely to be a pure exam factory.

🏆

Website shows only board toppers

Exam-factory mindset. Strong results — but creativity and openness may take a back seat.

👨‍🏫

Teacher retention above 5 years (Glassdoor)

Teachers feel valued. Stable classrooms. Your child won't get a new teacher every year.

🏗️

Founded by educator vs business group

Mission-driven vs profit-driven. Both can work — but the priorities show up in daily decisions.

📋

Parent committee with real influence

Management is accountable. Problems get addressed. Your voice as a parent will matter.

📈

Fee escalation above 12% per year

Revenue-first thinking. A ₹1.2L school today could be ₹2L in four years. We flag this risk.

What we found (and fixed)

During our research, we found several cases where widely-repeated claims didn't match reality. Here are a few examples — without naming specifics — of the kind of corrections that made it into our final data:

A popular school claimed an 11:1 student-teacher ratio

External verification on Ezyschooling showed the actual ratio at their second campus was 25:1 — more than double what parents expected. The main campus ratio checked out, but the newer campus hadn't maintained the same standard.

Score corrected ↓ by 33 points on ratio parameter

A well-known school listed as having "holistic campus with sports"

Multiple independent parent reviews revealed there is no playground on campus. Sports events happen at external venues. For a primary school child who needs daily outdoor play, this is a meaningful gap that wasn't visible on the school's website.

Infrastructure score corrected ↓ from 75 to 55

A school we initially scored as average turned out to be 23 years old

We had listed it as established in 2012 — but Ezyschooling showed 2003. We also found the actual student-teacher ratio was 16:1, not 22:1. Both corrections improved the school's ranking. Research doesn't always find bad news.

Score corrected ↑ — ratio and alumni scores both improved

Confidence ratings — what they mean

We're honest about what we know and what we don't. Every school gets a confidence rating based on how much independent verification we've been able to do:

High confidence
3+ independent sources agree. Google rating available with 50+ reviews. Claims cross-verified.
Medium confidence
1-2 independent sources. Some corroboration. Limited review volume or newer school with less data.
Low confidence
Mostly self-reported data. Few independent reviews. We've applied our signal framework but couldn't fully verify.

We'd rather tell you "we're not sure about this school" than pretend we know everything. You can filter by confidence level on the comparison page.

What we don't do

Honesty about limitations matters as much as the research itself.

  • We don't visit schools in person (yet). Our data comes from public sources, parent reviews, and institutional databases. A physical visit would add another layer — but it wouldn't scale to 62 schools.
  • We don't accept payments from schools. No school can pay to improve their ranking or remove a red flag. This tool is funded by the parents who built it.
  • We don't claim our scores are absolute truth. They're informed estimates based on the best available data. When we're uncertain, we say so.
  • We don't have access to every school's internal data. Some smaller schools have very limited public information — their scores carry a "Low confidence" tag for this reason.
  • We update as we learn. If a school contacts us with corrections, or a parent shares new information, we verify and update. The data is living, not frozen.

Why the weights matter more than the scores

Two families looking at the same school can have completely different experiences — because they value different things. A family that prioritizes a small class size will rank schools differently than a family focused on board results or budget.

That's why we built the tool around adjustable weights rather than a single "best school" list. The scores are our research. The weights are your priorities. Together, they give you a ranking that actually reflects what matters to your family.

Every red flag, every source citation, every implied signal interpretation is visible on each school's detail card. We're not asking you to trust us blindly — we're showing our work so you can decide for yourself.
